PHYSICS DEEP DIVE

1. How Rockets Work: Breaking Free

Rockets are the delivery vehicles of the cosmos. Their primary job is to fight Earth's gravity and atmospheric drag to deliver a payload (like a satellite) into orbit.

The Core Physics: Action and Reaction

Rockets operate entirely on Newton’s Third Law of Motion: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

  • The Action: The rocket engine burns fuel to create high-pressure, hot gas, which is expelled downward through a nozzle at supersonic speeds.
  • The Reaction: The upward force exerted on the rocket, known as thrust.

Unlike airplanes, rockets don't have the luxury of scooping up oxygen from the atmosphere to burn their fuel. Because space is a vacuum, a rocket must carry both its fuel (like liquid hydrogen or kerosene) and its oxidizer (liquid oxygen). Together, these are called propellants.

The Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation

The ultimate rule of rocketry is governed by the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation:

$$\Delta v = v_e \ln \frac{m_0}{m_f}$$

Where Δv (Delta-v) is the maximum change in velocity of the rocket, ve is the effective exhaust velocity, m0 is the initial total mass (including propellant), and mf is the final dry mass (without propellant).

The Rocket Equation Fact: This equation reveals a brutal reality: to get more speed, you need more fuel, but adding more fuel makes the rocket heavier, requiring even more fuel. Because of this, roughly 85% to 90% of a rocket's total mass at launch is just fuel.

Staging: Shedding Dead Weight

To beat the limits of the rocket equation, engineers use staging. A large rocket is actually two or three smaller rockets stacked on top of each other. Once the bottom stage runs out of fuel, it detaches and falls away (or lands, in the case of SpaceX). This instantly drops massive amounts of dead weight, allowing the smaller upper stage to accelerate much more efficiently.
