2. How Satellites Work: Falling Perpetually

Once a rocket drops a satellite off in space, the satellite doesn't just hover there. It stays in space by entering orbit.

The Physics of Orbit: The "Cannonball" Concept

The easiest way to understand an orbit is a thought experiment from Isaac Newton. Imagine a cannon on top of a tall mountain firing a cannonball horizontally:

  • If you fire it slowly, gravity pulls it down, and it hits the Earth.
  • If you fire it faster, it travels further before hitting the ground.
  • If you fire it exactly fast enough, the curve of the cannonball's falling path matches the curve of the Earth's surface.

A satellite is essentially a projectile in a constant state of free-fall around the Earth, but it is moving so fast sideways that it keeps missing the planet.

Orbital Mechanics Equation

The velocity (v) required to maintain a stable circular orbit depends entirely on the mass of the planet (M) and the distance from the center of the planet (r):

v = sqrt( GM / r )

Where G is the gravitational constant (6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² / kg²).

Orbital Speed Fact: To stay in LEO at ~400 km (where the ISS sits), a satellite must travel at roughly 7.66 km/s (~27,600 km/h). At this speed, it circles the entire Earth in just 90 minutes.

Key Types of Orbits

  • Low Earth Orbit (LEO): 160–2,000 km. Ideal for imaging, spy satellites, and science (e.g., Hubble, ISS).
  • Geostationary Orbit (GEO): Exactly 35,786 km above the equator. The orbital period matches Earth's 24-hour rotation — the satellite appears stationary in the sky. Perfect for weather and TV satellites.

3. The Essential Anatomy of a Satellite

  • Power System: Solar panels + batteries for power during Earth's shadow.
  • Attitude Control: Reaction wheels (spinning flywheels) or gas thrusters to rotate and orient antennas.
  • Communications: Transponders, antennas, and receivers.
  • Thermal Control: Multi-layer gold foil insulation blankets (MLI) and heat pipes to manage extreme temperature swings.
